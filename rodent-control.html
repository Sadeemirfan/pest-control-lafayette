Identifying Rodents Common to Lafayette & Acadiana

Lafayette and the broader Acadiana region's warm, humid subtropical climate creates ideal conditions for rodent populations to thrive year-round. Unlike colder northern states where harsh winters naturally suppress rodent activity, Louisiana's mild temperatures and abundant moisture mean rats and mice can breed continuously Ã¢ÂÂ producing several litters per year. If you suspect a rodent problem in your Lafayette home or business, identifying which species you're dealing with is the critical first step toward effective control.

Norway Rats (Rattus norvegicus)

Also called brown rats or sewer rats, Norway rats are the largest commensal rodent you're likely to encounter in Acadiana. Adults typically weigh 7Ã¢ÂÂ18 ounces and measure 13Ã¢ÂÂ18 inches from nose to tail tip. Their blunt muzzles, small ears, and scaly tails shorter than their body length distinguish them from other species. Norway rats are exceptional burrowers Ã¢ÂÂ they excavate tunnels beneath slabs, along foundation walls, under garden sheds, and throughout crawl spaces. In Lafayette neighborhoods, they're frequently found along drainage ditches, beneath concrete pads, and burrowing into the embankments adjacent to the Vermilion River and its tributaries. Norway rats are neophobic, meaning they're cautious around new objects, which makes baiting and trapping them somewhat more challenging.

They are exceptional climbers, ascending trees, power lines, fence rails, and downspouts with ease before gaining entry to attics, soffits, and wall voids through openings as small as a half-inch in diameter. Lafayette's mature oak and magnolia canopies, combined with dense shrubbery common in residential neighborhoods, create perfect highways for roof rats to move between properties. They are largely nocturnal, and homeowners most frequently become aware of them through scratching, scurrying, and rolling sounds in attics and ceilings during the night hours.

In Lafayette homes, mice commonly inhabit kitchen cabinets, pantries, utility closets, garage areas, and wall voids near heat sources like water heaters and HVAC equipment.

  • Gnaw marks: Rodents must continuously gnaw to control the growth of their incisors. Look for chew marks on food packaging, wooden structures, electrical wiring insulation, PVC pipes, and drywall.

Health Risks & Property Damage Caused by Rodents

Rodents are far more than a nuisance. They represent genuine, documented threats to the health of your family and the structural integrity of your property. In Lafayette and throughout Acadiana, the risks are amplified by our warm, wet climate that supports year-round rodent breeding and activity.

⚠

Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome

Hantavirus is transmitted primarily through contact with the urine, droppings, or saliva of infected rodents Ã¢ÂÂ particularly deer mice, though multiple species can carry the virus. Even inhaling dust contaminated with rodent excreta can transmit the disease. Hantavirus Pulmonary Syndrome (HPS) has a mortality rate of approximately 38% and causes severe respiratory distress. While cases in Louisiana are less common than in western states, the risk is real and should not be dismissed, particularly during attic cleanouts or when disturbing areas of heavy rodent activity.

⚠

Salmonellosis

Rodents are prolific carriers of Salmonella bacteria, contaminating food preparation surfaces, stored pantry items, and cooking utensils with their droppings, urine, and fur. In Louisiana, where food culture is deeply embedded in daily life, the contamination of kitchens by rodents is a particularly serious concern. A single mouse produces approximately 50Ã¢ÂÂ75 droppings per day, and they urinate constantly as they travel Ã¢ÂÂ making any area they inhabit heavily contaminated. Symptoms of Salmonellosis include severe diarrhea, abdominal cramps, fever, and vomiting, and the illness can be life-threatening for young children, elderly individuals, and immunocompromised persons.

⚠

Leptospirosis

Leptospirosis is a bacterial disease spread through contact with water, soil, or food contaminated by the urine of infected animals Ã¢ÂÂ most commonly rats. Given that Lafayette and Acadiana experience frequent flooding and have abundant bayous, drainage canals, and low-lying areas, leptospirosis is a genuine local health concern. After significant rain events or flooding, the risk increases substantially as rising water disperses rat urine across previously uncontaminated surfaces. Leptospirosis can cause kidney damage, meningitis, liver failure, and respiratory complications, and in severe cases (Weil's disease) can be fatal.

⚠

Rat-Bite Fever & Secondary Infections

Rat-bite fever is caused by bacteria transmitted through rat bites, scratches, or contact with rodent secretions. While relatively rare, it causes fever, rashes, and joint pain and requires prompt antibiotic treatment. Additionally, rodents carry fleas, ticks, and mites that can infest your home and transmit their own suite of diseases including plague (historically spread by rat fleas), murine typhus, and Lyme disease. In south Louisiana, where tick populations are already significant, rodent-associated ectoparasite introductions can compound existing pest pressures considerably.

⚡

Electrical Fire Risk

One of the most underappreciated dangers of rodent infestations is the risk of electrical fires. Rats and mice must constantly gnaw to maintain their ever-growing incisors, and they frequently target electrical wiring Ã¢ÂÂ stripping insulation from wires throughout attics, walls, and crawl spaces. The National Pest Management Association estimates that rodents may be responsible for 20Ã¢ÂÂ25% of fires of undetermined origin in the United States. In older Lafayette homes where wiring may already be aging, rodent damage to electrical systems creates a compounded risk. Many homeowners discover rat-damaged wiring only after a professional pest inspection or during renovation work.

⚡

Structural & Property Damage

Beyond electrical systems, rodents cause extensive structural damage throughout Acadiana homes. Norway rats burrow beneath foundations and slabs, compromising structural integrity over time. Roof rats shred attic insulation to build nests, dramatically reducing thermal efficiency and increasing energy costs. Both species gnaw through PVC plumbing pipes, causing water leaks and the associated mold risks that are already a significant concern in Louisiana's humid climate. Mice and rats contaminate insulation with urine and feces to the point where full attic remediation Ã¢ÂÂ including removal and replacement of all insulation Ã¢ÂÂ becomes necessary. These remediation costs typically run into thousands of dollars and are often not covered by standard homeowner's insurance policies.
